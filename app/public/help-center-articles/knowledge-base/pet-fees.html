What Pet Fees Do Landlords Charge?

Renting with a dog or cat can be expensive, thanks to a variety of pet-specific fees.

Renting with a pet involves several potential fees that landlords may charge. Understanding these costs upfront can help you budget for your furry companion.

Types of Pet Fees

1. Pet Deposit

A pet deposit is a one-time, refundable fee charged at move-in. This deposit covers potential damage caused by your pet. Typical amounts range from $200-$500.

  • Refundable if there's no pet damage
  • May be partial or full refund
  • Separate from security deposit

2. Pet Fee (Non-Refundable)

A one-time, non-refundable fee charged when you move in with a pet. Typical amounts range from $200-$500.

  • Not returned at move-out
  • Compensates for general wear and tear
  • May cover carpet cleaning

3. Monthly Pet Rent

An additional monthly charge on top of your regular rent. Typical amounts range from $25-$75 per month, per pet.

  • Recurring monthly expense
  • Not refundable
  • Can add up significantly over time

Example Annual Cost

Pet deposit: $300 + Pet fee: $300 + Pet rent ($50/month x 12): $600 = $1,200 in the first year

What Affects Pet Fee Amounts?

  • Size of pet: Larger dogs typically cost more
  • Number of pets: Multiple pets = higher fees
  • Type of property: Luxury apartments may charge more
  • Local market: Urban areas often have higher pet fees
  • Breed restrictions: Some breeds face additional scrutiny or fees

State Regulations

Some states limit how much landlords can charge in total deposits (including pet deposits), while others have no restrictions. Always check your state's landlord-tenant laws.

Service and Emotional Support Animals

Under the Fair Housing Act, landlords cannot charge pet fees or deposits for certified service animals or emotional support animals. These are considered accommodations, not pets.

Negotiating Pet Fees

  • Offer pet references from previous landlords
  • Provide proof of pet training or certification
  • Show vet records proving vaccinations and health
  • Offer to pay higher security deposit instead of monthly pet rent
  • Get renters insurance that covers pet damage
